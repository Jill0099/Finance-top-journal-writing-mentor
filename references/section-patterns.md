# Section-by-Section Writing Patterns
## Top Finance Journals: JFQA, JCF, Journal of Finance

Extracted from systematic reading of 50 papers + structured bib annotations.

---

## Abstract

### JFQA Pattern (100–140 words, 4 moves)

**Move 1 — Setup (1 sentence)**: Name the empirical phenomenon and agent.
> *"We examine whether political corruption impedes innovation."* (Ellis et al. 2020)
> *"We show that the 2016 Brexit Referendum had multifaceted consequences for corporate America."* (Campello et al. 2022)

**Move 2 — Method + Main finding (1–2 sentences)**: Name the empirical strategy and state the key result with direction.
> *"Using a comprehensive sample of U.S. firms, we find that corruption has a substantial, negative relation with the quantity and quality of innovation."* (Ellis et al. 2020)

**Move 3 — Robustness (1 sentence)**:
> *"These results are robust to using various fixed effects, proxies for corruption and innovation, and subsamples."* (Ellis et al. 2020)

**Move 4 — Identification OR interpretation (1–2 sentences)**:
> *"To establish causality, we employ 2 instruments for corruption: local ethnic diversity and the corruption of the state a firm's founder grew up in."* (Ellis et al. 2020)
> *"Overall, this evidence is consistent with the notion that corruption reduces social welfare by impeding innovation."*

### JCF Pattern (130–160 words, 5 moves)
Has an Article Info box on page 1 (Editor, JEL codes, Keywords). Abstract is slightly longer.

**Move 1 — Institutional setting / quasi-experiment**:
> *"Exploiting the executive compensation reform for state-owned enterprises (SOEs) in China that enforce strict pay restrictions, this study examines whether and how pay restrictions affect firms' labor investment inefficiency."* (Cao et al. 2026)

**Move 2–3 — Main findings with numbers**:
> *"We find that SOEs experience a decrease in abnormal labor investment following the reform relative to non-SOEs, particularly in over-investment in labor. Our results show that the reform is associated with lower labor investment inefficiency through strengthened internal governance and mitigated internal social comparison."*

**Move 4 — Mechanism/additional analysis**:
> *"In addition, pay restrictions specifically curb firms' tendency to over-hire."*

**Move 5 — Policy implication**:
> *"This study offers novel policy insights by showing how pay restrictions to SOE executives can reduce vertical agency costs and investment inefficiency."*

### JF Pattern (Presidential Address style)
Opens with a paradigm-challenge thesis sentence:
> *"Discount-rate variation is the central organizing question of current asset-pricing research."* (Cochrane 2011)

## Introduction

### Opening Sentence Archetypes

**Event hook** (strongest opening for empirical papers):
> *"On June 23, 2016, voters in the United Kingdom elected to leave the European Union."* (Campello et al. 2022)

**Empirical fact hook**:
> *"Policy uncertainty can harm the economy."* (Nguyen & Phan 2017)
> Followed immediately by: *"Stock and Watson (2012), Julio and Yook (2012), and Gulen and Ion (2016) argue that uncertainty related to tax, government spending, and regulatory and monetary policies hampers the post–Great Recession economic recovery."*

**Broad importance + seminal citations**:
> *"Economists understand the crucial role that innovation plays in a nation's economic growth (e.g., Schumpeter (1934), Solow (1957), and Romer (1990)). Less well understood, however, is what those nations might do to foster innovation."* (Ellis et al. 2020)

**Bold assertion (JF)**:
> *"Asset prices should equal expected discounted cashflows."* (Cochrane 2011)

**Never acceptable openings**:
- "In recent years..."
- "With the development of..."
- "This paper studies the relationship between..."
- "Over the past decade..."

### Gap Sentence Patterns

The gap sentence must be **explicit**, not implied:
> *"We fill this gap in the literature by studying how political corruption in the United States affects firm innovation."* (Ellis et al. 2020)
> *"However, no prior study has investigated the link between policy uncertainty and mergers and acquisitions (M&As), one of the most important forms of corporate investments."* (Nguyen & Phan 2017)
> *"Despite this body of evidence, the question of how foreign political shocks transmit across borders remains poorly understood."* (Campello et al. 2022)

### Identification Paragraph (¶3) — Most Critical

This paragraph must answer: **What prevents a confound from explaining your result?**

> *"To establish identification, we develop a neighboring-states method that uses bordering states without elections as a control group."* (Çolak et al. 2017)
> *"We use the 2014 pay restriction policy targeting SOEs as a quasi-natural experiment and compare restricted SOEs to unaffected non-SOEs before and after 2014."* (Lan & Pan 2026)
> *"We believe that the U.S.-only setting provides a better test of the relation between corruption and innovation than a cross-country design, because it avoids cross-country differences that might be correlated with both corruption and innovation."* (Ellis et al. 2020)

### Findings Preview — With Economic Magnitude

**Template**: coefficient direction → specific % → scale to baseline → baseline anchor.

> *"Our base difference-in-differences estimates show that in the last two quarters of 2016 alone, the investment-to-assets rates of U.K.-exposed firms fell by 0.16 percentage points more than the investment rates of comparable non-U.K.-exposed firms. Given that the average quarterly investment rate in 2015 was 1.1% of assets, this decline represents a drop of 15% in baseline investment spending."* (Campello et al. 2022)

> *"An interquartile increase in our primary measure of corruption is associated with a 17.4% decline in the number of patents (in its logarithmic form) and a 15.2% decline in the number of citations."* (Ellis et al. 2020)

### Robustness Preview (¶6)

> *"We conduct a range of robustness tests. First, we test the parallel trend assumption underpinning the validity of a DiD design. Second, to address concerns that changes in abnormal labor investment may be attributed to firm-specific characteristics, we employ the entropy-balancing approach. Third, we conduct placebo tests. Across all tests, we find that our baseline results are robust."* (Cao et al. 2026)

### Contribution Paragraph (¶7)

Template: name specific literatures → state what prior work did → state how this paper differs.

> *"We contribute to the literatures on both innovation and corruption and, more broadly, on how law and finance affect firms' real outcomes. Specifically, we add to the literature on how non-firm factors might impact innovation... One particularly related paper is Ovtchinnikov et al. (2020), who find that political activism fosters firm innovation... While their findings suggest that some firms can exploit political intelligence to become better innovators, our evidence indicates that these benefits are not any more likely to accrue to firms operating in a corrupt environment."* (Ellis et al. 2020)

### Roadmap Sentence

Always the **final sentence** of the introduction:
> *"The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section II reviews the related literature. Section III describes the data. Section IV presents the main results. Section V presents robustness tests. Section VI concludes."*

## Section II: Literature Review and Hypothesis Development

### JFQA: Combined section, prose hypotheses

Hypothesis stated as italicized prose deduction from a named theory:
> *"These arguments lead us to hypothesize that political corruption will reduce the quantity and quality of firm innovation, which we term 'the costly corruption hypothesis.'"* (Ellis et al. 2020)

Structure:
1. Review related literature (organized by strand)
2. State theoretical mechanism
3. Derive the testable prediction (italicized hypothesis)
4. State cross-sectional predictions (moderating conditions)

### JCF: Separate subsection "Institutional Background and Hypothesis Development"

Format:
```
2. Institutional background and hypothesis development
2.1. [Institutional context]
2.2. Hypothesis development

H1(a). [Formal directional statement.]
H1(b). [Formal directional statement.]
H2. [Second main hypothesis.]
```

Real example (Lan & Pan 2026):
> **H1(a).** The 2014 policy reduces the level of executive pay and perk expenses in SOEs.
> **H1(b).** The 2014 policy reduces the pay-performance sensitivity of executive compensation in SOEs.

Each hypothesis preceded by 1–2 paragraphs of theory. Hypothesis stated as expected directional relationship ("reduces," "increases," "is positively associated with").

## Data Section

### Standard subsection structure

```
3. Data and Variables
3.1. Sample Construction
3.2. Main Variables
    3.2.1. Dependent Variable
    3.2.2. Key Independent Variable
    3.2.3. Control Variables
3.3. Descriptive Statistics
```

### Variable definition conventions

> *"We obtain [variable name] from [data source], which covers [scope/period]. Specifically, [variable] is defined as [exact definition]. Following [prior paper], we winsorize [variable] at the [X]th percentile to mitigate the influence of outliers."*

Data sources always cited: Compustat, CRSP, ExecuComp, Thomson Reuters, Wind, CSMAR, FEC.

### Descriptive statistics table

- Table 1 always presents summary statistics: N, mean, standard deviation, P25, median, P75
- Followed by a correlation matrix or pairwise correlation discussion
- "Table 1 reports summary statistics. The mean [dependent variable] is [X], with a standard deviation of [Y]."

## Main Results Section

### Regression table conventions

- Baseline model in Column (1); add controls progressively
- Fixed effects disclosed in footer rows
- Standard errors: "robust standard errors clustered at the [firm / industry / state] level"
- Significance stars: *** p<0.01, ** p<0.05, * p<0.10

### Prose reporting pattern

> *"Column (1) of Table 2 reports the results of our baseline regression... The coefficient on [variable] is [sign and magnitude] and statistically significant at the [1/5/10]% level (t-statistic = X). This result suggests that a one-standard-deviation increase in [X] is associated with a [magnitude] [increase/decrease] in [Y], which represents approximately [Z]% of the sample mean."*

### Economic significance convention

Always anchor to the sample mean or a meaningful interquartile range:
> *"An interquartile increase in [X] corresponds to a [magnitude] change in [Y], representing a [Z]% change relative to the unconditional mean of [Z]."*

## Mechanism / Channel Tests

**Separate from robustness tests.** Label: "Mechanism Analysis" or "Additional Tests."

### Cross-sectional heterogeneity pattern

> *"To investigate the mechanism, we split the sample into [high/low X] subgroups and re-estimate our baseline regression. Consistent with the [channel] hypothesis, the effect is concentrated among firms with [high X] (coefficient = [X], t = [Y]), while the effect is statistically insignificant for firms with [low X] (coefficient = [X], t = [Y])."*

### Interaction term pattern

> *"We augment the baseline regression with an interaction term between [treatment] and [moderator]. The positive and significant coefficient on the interaction term (coefficient = [X], t = [Y]) indicates that the effect is stronger when [moderating condition], consistent with [theory]."*

## Robustness Section

Standard sequence (from Cao et al. 2026):
1. Parallel trends test / dynamic event study plot
2. Placebo test (pseudo-treatment dates)
3. Alternative measures of key variables
4. Alternative fixed effects specifications
5. Entropy balancing / PSM-DiD
6. Oster (2019) bound estimate for omitted variable bias
7. Sub-sample analysis (drop [specific concern group])

### Parallel trends reporting

> *"We verify the parallel trends assumption by estimating a dynamic event study specification. Figure X plots the estimated coefficients for each year relative to the policy implementation year (t = 0). The pre-period coefficients are all statistically indistinguishable from zero (joint F-test p-value = [X]), while the post-period coefficients show a significant decline, consistent with our baseline results."*

## Conclusion

### Standard length: 1–2 pages

### Structure:
1. Summary of main question and contribution (1 paragraph)
2. Summary of main findings (1 paragraph)
3. Policy implications (1 paragraph, optional)
4. Limitations / future directions (1 paragraph)

### Hedging in conclusion

The conclusion always restates findings with hedged language:
> *"Our results suggest that...", "Our evidence is consistent with..."*

Never: "We have proven that..." or "Our study conclusively demonstrates..."
