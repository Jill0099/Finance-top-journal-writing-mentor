# How Top Papers Sell Themselves: 8 Rhetorical Strategies

Distilled from introductions of 50+ papers published in JFQA, JCF, Journal of Finance, and Review of Finance. These strategies explain not just *what* makes a paper publishable, but *how* successful authors convince editors and referees their work matters.

---

## Why "Selling" Matters

Editors typically decide by the end of the introduction (Edmans, 2023). The same research idea, framed differently, can receive either a desk rejection or an R&R. These strategies are not about spin — they are about making the paper's genuine contribution *legible* to time-constrained readers.

---

## Strategy 1: The "First Evidence" Claim (Scoped Carefully)

**What it is**: An explicit novelty claim, but narrowly scoped to be defensible.

**Why it works**: Editors screen hundreds of papers. An unambiguous novelty claim signals this isn't incremental work.

**Examples**:
- Khan et al. (HBS): **"First Evidence on Materiality"** — in the title itself
- Hutton et al. (2014, JFQA): *"To our knowledge, this is the first study that directly measures political preferences of a large, comprehensive sample of corporate managers and shows that political preferences help explain leverage choices and investment policies."*
- Lan & Pan (2026, JCF): *"We provide the first empirical evidence on how pay restrictions influence major corporate investment decisions."*

**How to execute**:
1. Verify the claim — search thoroughly to confirm no prior paper does exactly what you do
2. Be precise about *what* is first (not "first to study X" broadly, but "first to use method M on question Q in setting S")
3. Place the claim in both the abstract and the introduction
4. Scope it down if needed: "first study to *directly measure*..." or "first *comprehensive* sample..."

**Red flag**: Overclaiming. "First" claims that a referee can contradict will sink the paper.

## Strategy 2: The "Known A + Known B, Nobody Connected Them" Gap

**What it is**: Two established literatures exist but the intersection is unstudied. Your paper fills the gap.

**Why it works**: The gap is obviously real (both literatures exist), and the contribution feels large because it bridges two active research areas.

**Examples**:
- Ellis et al. (2020, JFQA): Corruption affects growth (known) + Innovation drives growth (known) → *"However, despite the prevalence of rent-seeking by public officials around the world and the consensus that corruption affects growth, few empirical studies have examined the relation between corruption and innovation. We fill this gap."*
- Nguyen & Phan (2017, JFQA): Policy uncertainty reduces investment (known) + M&As are major investments (known) → *"However, no prior study has investigated the link between policy uncertainty and mergers and acquisitions (M&As), one of the most important forms of corporate investments."*

**How to execute**:
1. Establish both literatures in ¶1-2 with seminal citations
2. State the gap explicitly — "We fill this gap" or "no prior study has examined"
3. Explain *why* the connection matters (it's not obvious that corruption→innovation just because corruption→growth and innovation→growth)

**Red flag**: If the connection is trivially predicted by chaining known results (Edmans' "convex combination" sin), the gap feels artificial.

## Strategy 3: Turning a Setting Limitation into an Identification Advantage

**What it is**: Reframe what could seem like a narrow empirical context as methodologically *superior* to alternatives.

**Why it works**: It defuses the "why this country/period?" question and turns it into a strength.

**Examples**:
- Ellis et al. (2020, JFQA): *"We believe that the U.S.-only setting provides a better test of the relation between corruption and innovation than a cross-country design, because it avoids cross-country differences that might be correlated with both corruption and innovation."*
- Campello et al. (2022, JFQA): Studying U.S. firms (not U.K.) means effects are *"plausibly not"* endogenous to the referendum itself
- Lan & Pan (2026, JCF): China's *"directly restrictive"* pay policy vs. the U.S. "$1 million rule" which *"did not achieve their objective"*

**How to execute**:
1. Name the limitation explicitly (single country, specific policy)
2. Contrast with the alternative setting's weaknesses
3. Explain why your setting generates *cleaner* identification for *this specific question*

**Red flag**: "Ideal setting" claims without specifics about *what* makes it ideal for *this* question.

---

## Strategy 4: The Endogeneity Critique of All Prior Work

**What it is**: Dismiss an entire literature as correlational, then offer a causal design.

**Why it works**: Causal credibility is the #1 concern at top finance journals. Claiming your design solves the identification problem that 100+ prior papers could not is a powerful positioning move.

**Examples**:
- Flammer (2015, MS): *"167 studies have examined the CSR-CFP link... A limitation of this literature is that CSR is endogenous with respect to CFP... the positive correlation that emerges from the literature, albeit interesting, does not warrant a causal interpretation."*
- Khan et al. (HBS): *"The results from this line of research still remain mixed"* → introduces materiality distinction as the resolution
- Cao et al. (2026, JCF): *"The effect of pay regulation is thus an empirical question that warrants further investigation"*

**How to execute**:
1. Quantify the prior literature if possible ("167 studies", "no fewer than 16 review articles")
2. Name the specific endogeneity concern (reverse causality, omitted variable, self-selection)
3. Explain why your design resolves it
4. Be honest about remaining limitations — overconfidence in causal claims backfires

**Red flag**: Claiming to "solve" endogeneity when your design has obvious holes.

## Strategy 5: The Dramatic / Vivid Opening Hook

**What it is**: Start with a concrete event, statistic, or anecdote rather than abstract theory.

**Why it works**: Captures attention and establishes real-world relevance in the first sentence. The reader is immediately grounded in something tangible.

**Examples**:
- Campello et al. (2022, JFQA): *"On June 23, 2016, voters in the United Kingdom elected to leave the European Union. The outcome of the Brexit referendum was surprising since most opinion polls had the 'remain' vote winning by safe margins."*
- Hutton et al. (2014, JFQA): *"Conservatism is one of the hallmarks of the Republican Party. According to a recent Gallup poll, 73% of Republicans identify themselves as conservative."*
- Flammer (2015, MS): Opens with Marks & Spencer's CSR yielding *"185 million in net benefits"*

**How to execute**:
1. Choose a *specific* hook (named company, exact date, precise number) — not generic
2. Keep it to 1-2 sentences — don't linger
3. Transition with contrast: "Despite this evidence, the academic literature has not..."
4. The hook must connect directly to the research question — no clickbait

**Never acceptable**: "In recent years...", "With the development of...", "This paper studies..."

## Strategy 6: The "Ideal Experiment" Approximation

**What it is**: Describe what the perfect experiment would look like, then claim your design approximates it.

**Why it works**: Makes the identification logic transparent and signals methodological sophistication.

**Examples**:
- Flammer (2015, MS): *"The ideal experiment would be to randomly assign firms to either adopt a certain CSR activity or not... Obviously, such an ideal experiment would be difficult... In this paper, I consider a quasi-natural experiment that is very close in spirit to this ideal experiment."*
- Cao et al. (2026, JCF): *"This reform, as a quasi-natural experiment, provides an ideal setting to unlock the black box."*

**How to execute**:
1. State the ideal experiment in one sentence
2. Acknowledge it's impossible ("obviously, such an experiment would be difficult")
3. Present your design as the closest feasible approximation
4. Explain which assumptions are needed to bridge the gap

**Red flag**: Claiming your setting is "ideal" when the treatment is anticipated or endogenous.

## Strategy 7: Explicit Differentiation from the Closest Rival Paper

**What it is**: Name the most similar published paper and explain precisely how yours differs.

**Why it works**: Referees will compare you to the closest paper regardless — better to control the comparison yourself.

**Examples**:
- Nguyen & Phan (2017, JFQA): Dedicates a full paragraph distinguishing from Gulen & Ion (different investment type) and Bhagwat et al. (different uncertainty measure)
- Ellis et al. (2020, JFQA): *"One particularly related paper is Ovtchinnikov et al. (2020), who find that political activism fosters firm innovation... While their findings suggest that some firms can exploit political intelligence to become better innovators, our evidence indicates that these benefits are not any more likely to accrue to firms operating in a corrupt environment."*
- Campello et al. (2022, JFQA): Differentiates from Hassan et al. by using 10-K filings rather than conference calls

**How to execute**:
1. Name the 1-2 closest papers explicitly in the contribution paragraph
2. State what they did
3. State what you do differently (different DV, different design, different mechanism)
4. Tone: respectful but clear — "related but distinct" rather than "they are wrong"

---

## Strategy 8: Policy Relevance / Real-World Stakes Framing

**What it is**: Connect findings to policymakers, practitioners, or investors beyond academic contribution.

**Why it works**: Broadens the paper's appeal and passes the "so what?" test for non-specialist readers. Editors value papers that matter beyond the journal.

**Examples**:
- Nguyen & Phan (2017, JFQA): *"Our research findings can have timely implications for policy and corporate decision makers, which can be particularly useful given the recent wide swings in policy uncertainty."*
- Cao et al. (2026, JCF): *"This study offers novel policy insights by showing how pay restrictions to SOE executives can reduce vertical agency costs."*
- Khan et al. (HBS): *"Results are also likely to be of interest to firms making sustainability investment decisions, and to investors making portfolio allocation decisions."*

**How to execute**:
1. Place in the contribution paragraph or the abstract's final sentence
2. Be specific about *who* benefits (regulators, investors, boards) — not just "policymakers"
3. State what *action* they should take based on your findings
4. Don't overstate — "our findings suggest" not "policymakers should immediately"

## Combining Strategies

Top papers use 2-3 strategies together. Common combinations observed in the corpus:

| Combination | Example |
|-------------|---------|
| Gap + First Evidence | Ellis et al.: "few studies have examined...We fill this gap" + "first study" claim |
| Dramatic Hook + Ideal Setting | Campello et al.: Brexit event + U.S. firms as identification advantage |
| Endogeneity Critique + Ideal Experiment | Flammer: "167 studies are correlational" + RDD as approximation |
| First Evidence + Policy Relevance | Lan & Pan: "first evidence on pay restrictions and M&As" + policy implications |
| Gap + Rival Differentiation | Nguyen & Phan: "no prior study on uncertainty-M&A" + explicit comparison to Gulen & Ion |

**Rule of thumb**: Use 2-3 complementary strategies. Using all 8 in one introduction is overwhelming and signals insecurity.

## Evaluating Your Paper's "Sellability"

Before writing the introduction, answer these five questions:

1. **Can you state the contribution in one sentence?** If not, the paper tries to do too much.
2. **Which 2-3 strategies fit naturally?** If none fits, the contribution may be too incremental.
3. **What's the one memorable number?** The economic magnitude that a reader will remember and cite.
4. **Who is the implied reader?** Frame the sell for the journal's typical audience.
5. **Can a referee guess the result?** If yes, the "prior-changing" test (Edmans) fails — find a more surprising angle.
